Advanced English Grammar

Master the complexities of English grammar with these advanced concepts and structures.

1. Verb Tenses

English has 12 main verb tenses, each showing the time and completeness of an action.

Present Tenses

  • Simple Present: I write reports every day.
  • Present Continuous: I am writing a report now.
  • Present Perfect: I have written three reports this week.
  • Present Perfect Continuous: I have been writing reports since morning.

Past Tenses

  • Simple Past: I wrote a report yesterday.
  • Past Continuous: I was writing a report when you called.
  • Past Perfect: I had written the report before the meeting.
  • Past Perfect Continuous: I had been writing for two hours when you arrived.

Future Tenses

  • Simple Future: I will write a report tomorrow.
  • Future Continuous: I will be writing a report at 3 PM.
  • Future Perfect: I will have written the report by Friday.
  • Future Perfect Continuous: I will have been writing for three hours by 5 PM.

2. Conditionals

Conditional sentences express hypothetical situations and their consequences.

Type Structure Example Usage
Zero If + present, present If you heat ice, it melts. General truths
First If + present, will + base verb If it rains, I will stay home. Real future possibilities
Second If + past simple, would + base verb If I won the lottery, I would travel. Unreal present/future
Third If + past perfect, would have + past participle If I had studied, I would have passed. Unreal past
Note: For the second conditional, "were" is used with all subjects in formal English: "If I were you, I would..."

3. Reported Speech (Indirect Speech)

Reported speech is used to tell someone what another person said without using their exact words.

Basic Tense Changes:

Direct Speech Reported Speech Example
Present Simple Past Simple "I like ice cream." → He said (that) he liked ice cream.
Present Continuous Past Continuous "I am working." → She said (that) she was working.
Present Perfect Past Perfect "I have finished." → He said (that) he had finished.
Past Simple Past Perfect "I went home." → She said (that) she had gone home.
Will Would "I will help." → He said (that) he would help.
Can Could "I can swim." → She said (that) she could swim.
May Might "I may be late." → He said (that) he might be late.
Must/Have to Had to "I must go." → She said (that) she had to go.

Time and Place Changes:

Direct Speech Reported Speech
now then/at that time
today that day
yesterday the day before/the previous day
tomorrow the next/following day
next week the following week
last night the night before
this/these that/those
here there

Reporting Verbs and Their Patterns:

Different reporting verbs can be used to show the speaker's attitude or the way something was said:

  • Say + (that) + clause: "I'm tired," he said. → He said (that) he was tired.
  • Tell + object + (that) + clause: "I love you," she told me. → She told me (that) she loved me.
  • Ask + (object) + if/whether: "Are you coming?" → She asked (me) if I was coming.
  • Order/Command + object + to + base verb: "Sit down!" → He ordered me to sit down.
  • Advise + object + to + base verb: "You should study." → She advised me to study.

Special Cases:

1. Requests with 'would', 'could', 'can'

Direct: "Could you help me?" she asked.

Reported: She asked me to help her.

2. Suggestions with 'let's'

Direct: "Let's go to the cinema," he said.

Reported: He suggested going to the cinema. OR He suggested that we go to the cinema.

3. General truths

Direct: "The Earth revolves around the Sun," the teacher said.

Reported (no tense change): The teacher said that the Earth revolves around the Sun.

4. Reporting questions

Yes/No questions: "Are you coming?" → She asked if/whether I was coming.

Wh- questions: "Where do you live?" → He asked where I lived.

4. Complex Sentences

Complex sentences contain one independent clause and at least one dependent clause.

Types of Dependent Clauses:

  • Adverbial Clauses: Show when, where, why, how, or under what conditions something happens.
    After I finish work, I will go to the gym.
  • Adjective Clauses: Describe a noun (also called relative clauses).
    The book that I borrowed from the library is very interesting.
  • Noun Clauses: Function as a noun in the sentence.
    I know that you are telling the truth.

Subordinating Conjunctions:

These words introduce dependent clauses:

  • Time: when, while, after, before, since, until, as soon as
  • Cause/Effect: because, since, as, so that
  • Condition: if, unless, provided that, as long as
  • Contrast: although, though, even though, whereas, while
Tip: When a dependent clause comes before the independent clause, use a comma after it.
Because it was raining, we stayed inside.

5. Modal Verbs

Modal verbs are auxiliary verbs that express necessity, ability, permission, or possibility.

Common Modal Verbs:

Modal Usage Examples
Can/Could Ability, possibility, permission I can swim. / Could I borrow your pen?
May/Might Possibility, permission It may rain today. / May I leave early?
Must/Have to Necessity, obligation You must wear a seatbelt. / I have to finish this.
Shall/Should Suggestions, advice Shall we go? / You should see a doctor.
Will/Would Future, requests, habits I will help you. / Would you like some tea?
Remember: Modal verbs are followed by the base form of the main verb (without 'to').

6. Passive Voice

The passive voice is used when the focus is on the action rather than who performed it.

Formation:

Subject + be + past participle (by + agent)

Active: The chef prepared the meal.

Passive: The meal was prepared by the chef.

When to Use Passive Voice:

  • When the doer is unknown or unimportant
  • When the action is more important than the doer
  • In formal or scientific writing

Examples in Different Tenses:

Tense Active Passive
Present Simple They make cars in Japan. Cars are made in Japan.
Past Simple Shakespeare wrote Hamlet. Hamlet was written by Shakespeare.
Present Perfect Someone has stolen my bike. My bike has been stolen.
Future They will complete the project. The project will be completed.

7. Phrasal Verbs

Phrasal verbs are combinations of a verb and a particle (preposition or adverb) that create a new meaning.

Common Phrasal Verbs:

Phrasal Verb Meaning Example
Bring up Mention or raise a topic She brought up an interesting point.
Call off Cancel They called off the meeting.
Find out Discover information I need to find out the train times.
Give up Stop doing something He gave up smoking last year.
Look after Take care of Can you look after my cat?
Put off Postpone We had to put off our trip.
Take after Resemble a family member She takes after her mother.
Turn down Refuse an offer He turned down the job offer.
Tip: Some phrasal verbs are separable (you can put the object between the verb and particle), while others are not.

Separable: Turn off the light. / Turn the light off.

Not separable: I'll look after the children. (NOT: I'll look the children after.)

8. Common Grammar Mistakes

Avoid these common errors in English grammar.

1. Your vs. You're

Incorrect: Your going to love this movie.

Correct: You're going to love this movie.

2. Their/There/They're

Incorrect: Their going to the party later.

Correct: They're going to the party later.

3. Its vs. It's

Incorrect: The dog wagged it's tail.

Correct: The dog wagged its tail.

4. Fewer vs. Less

Incorrect: There are less people here today.

Correct: There are fewer people here today.

5. Affect vs. Effect

Incorrect: The medicine had no affect on him.

Correct: The medicine had no effect on him.

9. Advanced Punctuation

Master these punctuation marks for more sophisticated writing.

1. Semicolon (;)

Use to connect related independent clauses.

I have a meeting at 3:00; please don't be late.

2. Colon (:)

Introduces a list, explanation, or quotation.

She bought three things: apples, oranges, and bananas.

3. Em Dash (—)

Indicates a break in thought or adds emphasis.

The answer—though we didn't know it at the time—was right in front of us.

4. Parentheses ( )

Adds non-essential information.

The conference (which was held in Paris) was a great success.
