Pronunciation Rules

Spanish pronunciation is more consistent than English. Here are the key rules:

Vowels

Spanish has five pure vowel sounds that are always pronounced the same way:

  • A - always like 'a' in "father"
  • E - like 'e' in "they"
  • I - like 'ee' in "see"
  • O - like 'o' in "more"
  • U - like 'oo' in "moon"

Consonants

  • C - before e/i: 'th' in Spain, 's' in Latin America; otherwise 'k'
  • G - before e/i: like 'h' in "hello"; otherwise like 'g' in "go"
  • H - always silent
  • J - like a strong 'h' sound
  • LL - like 'y' in "yes" or 'j' in "jeep"
  • √ë - like 'ny' in "canyon"
  • R - tapped or rolled 'r' sound
  • RR - strongly rolled 'r'

Accent Marks

Spanish uses accent marks (tildes) to indicate stress and distinguish between words.

Rules for Accent Marks

  1. Words ending in a vowel, n, or s are stressed on the second-to-last syllable.
    • Example: casa (house), libros (books)
  2. Words ending in a consonant (except n or s) are stressed on the last syllable.
    • Example: espa√±ol (Spanish), verdad (truth)
  3. Accent marks are used when the word doesn't follow the above rules.

Noun Gender and Articles

In Spanish, all nouns have a gender (masculine or feminine) and number (singular or plural).

Definite Articles (The)

  • el (masculine singular) - the
  • la (feminine singular) - the
  • los (masculine plural) - the
  • las (feminine plural) - the

Indefinite Articles (A/An/Some)

  • un (masculine singular) - a/an
  • una (feminine singular) - a/an
  • unos (masculine plural) - some
  • unas (feminine plural) - some
Tip: Most nouns ending in -o are masculine, and most ending in -a are feminine, but there are exceptions!

Adjectives

Adjectives in Spanish must agree in gender and number with the nouns they modify.
